Most close teams track status.

Fewer teams quantify **risk** in a way that helps leaders decide where to intervene first.

That’s why issues that looked “yellow” on Monday become “red” by Wednesday.

AI can automate close risk scoring so teams identify fragile workflows early and reduce end-of-cycle escalation.

## Why close risk is hard to score manually

Manual scoring breaks down because:

- risk signals are spread across comments, tickets, spreadsheets, and inboxes
- severity is judged inconsistently across teams
- dependencies change daily as upstream tasks slip
- people under-report risk to avoid creating noise

Without a consistent model, risk reviews become subjective and slow.

## A practical AI workflow for close risk scoring

1. **Define a risk rubric for close tasks**
   Use weighted factors like due-date proximity, dependency depth, unresolved blockers, and owner capacity.

2. **Ingest signals from your close systems**
   Pull status updates, reopen events, late approvals, and blocked handoffs into one normalized stream.

3. **Apply dynamic risk scoring daily (or hourly in final week)**
   AI recalculates task-level risk whenever key conditions change.

4. **Escalate based on threshold + impact**
   A medium-risk task on the critical path should rank above a high-risk task with no downstream impact.

5. **Explain the score, not just the number**
   Every alert should include the top drivers (e.g., “2 upstream blockers + owner over-capacity + 1-day deadline”).

6. **Recommend concrete mitigations**
   Suggest owner reassignment, review sequencing changes, or temporary capacity rebalancing.

7. **Track score accuracy after close**
   Compare predicted risk vs actual delays to tune factor weights over time.

## Where to pilot first

Start with one close stream where misses are expensive:

- intercompany eliminations
- revenue recognition adjustments
- accrual review and approvals
- disclosure checklist completion

Measure over 2–3 cycles:

- high-risk alerts generated vs resolved
- critical-path delay hours prevented
- number of last-48-hour escalations

## Governance to keep risk scoring credible

- define a shared risk taxonomy and scoring definitions
- require human confirmation for high-impact escalations
- keep timestamped scoring history for auditability
- review false positives weekly and tune thresholds

## What good looks like

When close risk scoring is automated well, teams typically see:

- earlier visibility into where close will slip
- fewer surprise blockers in final close days
- faster escalation routing to the right owners
- more predictable close completion timelines

---

If close health reviews still rely on gut feel, risk scoring automation is one of the fastest ways to move from reactive firefighting to proactive control.
