If you record Zoom meetings, you already know the problem: the recordings pile up, nobody listens to them, and whoever needed the summary ends up asking you to recap everything anyway.

The solution is automatic transcription — but most people don't know you can do it without paying $30/month to Otter or $1.50/minute to Rev.

Here's the practical guide.

## What You Actually Get from Automatic Zoom Transcription

When you transcribe a Zoom recording automatically, you get:

- **A text transcript** with speaker labels (so you know who said what)
- **A searchable document** you can ctrl+F through
- **A base for AI summaries** — paste it into any AI tool for action items
- **A record** that protects you in disputes or compliance checks

The transcript is often more useful than the video itself.

## Option 1: Zoom's Built-In Transcription (Already Paid For)

If you have a Zoom Pro or higher plan, you already have cloud transcription included.

**How to enable it:**
1. Go to zoom.us → Settings → Recording
2. Enable "Cloud recording"
3. Enable "Audio transcript"
4. Record your next meeting to the cloud

After the meeting, Zoom emails you a link. You get `.vtt` and `.txt` transcript files alongside the video.

**The catch:** Zoom's transcription accuracy is mediocre — roughly 75-85% for clear speech, worse for accents, technical jargon, or anyone not speaking directly into a microphone. You'll spend time cleaning it up.

## Option 2: Upload to an AI Transcription Tool

For better accuracy, download your Zoom recording (`.mp4` or `.m4a`) and upload it to an AI transcription tool.

Good options in 2025:
- **Software Multi-Tool** — upload audio/video, get a transcript with speaker separation, then run a meeting summary automatically
- **AssemblyAI** — API-first, good accuracy, pay-per-minute pricing
- **Whisper** (OpenAI) — open source, runs locally, excellent accuracy

If you're doing this regularly for a team, a tool with a UI is easier than running scripts.

## Option 3: Batch Processing with Speaker Separation

For teams with weekly recurring meetings, the best workflow is:

1. Export Zoom recording to `.m4a` (audio only — smaller files)
2. Upload to a tool with speaker diarization (identifies who's speaking)
3. Get back a labeled transcript: "Speaker 1: Let's discuss the Q2 roadmap..."
4. Run that through a meeting summarizer to get action items

Speaker separation is the feature that makes transcripts actually readable. Without it, you get one giant block of text.

Software Multi-Tool's [Speaker Separation tool](/tools/speaker-separation) handles both steps — you upload the audio file and get back a diarized transcript ready for summarization.

## Option 4: Real-Time Transcription During the Meeting

If you want the transcript without downloading and uploading files:

- **Zoom's live captions** — free on Pro+, visible during the meeting
- **Otter.ai bot** — joins as a Zoom participant, transcribes live (paid)
- **Fireflies.ai** — similar bot approach, good accuracy on Zoom

The trade-off: bots joining your Zoom call can feel awkward in client meetings. Many people prefer the post-processing approach.

## Turning Transcripts into Action Items

A transcript alone isn't actionable. The real value is summarization.

Once you have a transcript, paste it into your AI summarization tool and ask for:

- **Action items** with owners and deadlines
- **Key decisions made** in the meeting
- **Open questions** that need follow-up
- **Meeting summary** in 3-5 bullet points

## Common Problems and Fixes

**Problem: Names are transcribed wrong**
Fix: After getting the transcript, do a search-and-replace for known names. "John" might come out as "Jon" or a technical acronym might be spelled phonetically.

**Problem: Multiple speakers sound similar**
Fix: Use a tool with speaker diarization rather than just transcription. Diarization clusters speech by voice characteristics, even when names aren't spoken.

**Problem: Background noise kills accuracy**
Fix: Ask participants to mute when not speaking, or use audio cleaning before transcription. Background noise is the #1 accuracy killer.

**Problem: Files are too large to upload**
Fix: Convert `.mp4` to `.m4a` (audio only). A 1-hour Zoom recording drops from ~500MB to ~50MB in audio format.

## Setting Up a Repeatable Workflow

If you're doing this weekly:

1. **Standardize your Zoom recordings** — always record to cloud or always to local
2. **Name files consistently** — `2026-03-24-team-standup.m4a`
3. **Batch transcribe weekly** — collect all recordings from the week and process them together
4. **Store transcripts somewhere searchable** — Notion, Google Drive, or Confluence

Once the process is set up, each meeting takes about 2 minutes of post-meeting overhead instead of 30.
