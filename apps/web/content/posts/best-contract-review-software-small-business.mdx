Small businesses sign contracts constantly. Vendor agreements. Client contracts. SaaS terms. Employment offers. NDAs.

And most small business owners sign them the same way: skim, spot anything obviously bad, sign.

That's a real liability. Contract review software gives you a way to actually understand what you're signing — without a lawyer on retainer.

Here's what's available in 2025 for small businesses specifically.

## What to Look For in Contract Review Software

**For small businesses, the priorities are different from enterprise:**

- **No per-seat pricing that makes costs unpredictable** — a 5-person company can't afford enterprise contract software
- **No legal degree required** — the output needs to be in plain English
- **Fast turnaround** — you need the analysis before you sign, not 3 days later
- **File upload support** — PDFs, Word docs, whatever you got
- **Actionable output** — not just "this is a clause" but "this is risky because..."

## The Real Question: Do You Need Software or a Lawyer?

Contract review software is not a replacement for legal counsel on high-stakes contracts.

Use software for:
- Standard vendor agreements
- SaaS terms and conditions
- Basic NDAs
- Routine client contracts

Use a lawyer for:
- Anything with significant financial exposure ($50K+)
- Acquisition or investment agreements
- Employment contracts in jurisdictions with complex law
- Anything where you're negotiating significantly non-standard terms

The software gives you a first pass. You should still know when to escalate to a professional.

## What contract review software actually catches

In our experience, the most common issues AI contract reviewers surface:

**Auto-renewal clauses** — many small businesses get caught by contracts that renew automatically with 30-day notice requirements. Easy to miss in a long document.

**Limitation of liability caps** — contracts that cap the vendor's liability at 1x the annual contract value, which means if they cause a $500K problem you can only recover $5K (if your contract is $5K).

**Intellectual property assignment** — contractor agreements sometimes include IP assignment language that gives you less ownership than you think.

**Exclusivity and non-compete language** — vendor agreements sometimes limit who else you can work with without explicitly calling it an exclusivity clause.

**Indemnification imbalances** — where you're indemnifying them for everything but their reciprocal obligation is narrow.

None of these require a lawyer to spot once you know to look for them. Contract review software makes sure you actually look.
