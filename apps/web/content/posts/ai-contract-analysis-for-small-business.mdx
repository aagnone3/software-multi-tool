Most small businesses sign contracts they don't fully understand. Not because the owners are careless — but because having a lawyer review every vendor agreement, lease, or service contract isn't economically viable.

AI contract analysis changes the cost equation significantly.

## What Small Businesses Are Signing

Small business owners sign contracts constantly:

- **Vendor agreements**: Supply contracts, service agreements, software subscriptions
- **Commercial leases**: Office space, retail locations, storage
- **Employment agreements**: Offer letters, NDAs, non-competes
- **Client contracts**: Service agreements, statements of work, master service agreements
- **Loan documents**: Business loans, equipment financing, lines of credit
- **Partnership agreements**: With co-founders, JV partners, or resellers

Each one contains clauses that could cost the business money or create legal exposure if overlooked. Auto-renewal clauses, personal guarantees, limitation of liability waivers, payment terms, and termination penalties are common landmines.

## What AI Contract Analysis Actually Does

An AI contract analyzer reads the full text of a contract and surfaces:

**Key financial terms**: Payment amounts, due dates, late fees, price escalation provisions.

**Obligations and deadlines**: What you're required to do, and by when.

**Termination provisions**: How to exit the contract, notice requirements, and penalties for early termination.

**Auto-renewal clauses**: Contracts that automatically renew are a common source of unwanted multi-year commitments.

**Liability terms**: Caps on damages, indemnification obligations, and insurance requirements.

**Unusual or problematic clauses**: Language that deviates from standard practice in ways that favor the other party.

This doesn't replace legal counsel for high-stakes contracts. But it surfaces the issues that warrant legal attention and eliminates the need to pay for lawyer time on routine reviews.

## When to Use AI vs. When to Use a Lawyer

**Use AI contract analysis for:**
- Initial review of any contract before negotiation
- Vendor agreements under $10,000
- Renewals of existing contracts (check what changed)
- Understanding what you already signed
- Quickly comparing similar contracts from different vendors

**Use a lawyer for:**
- Contracts over $50,000
- Employment agreements with significant equity components
- Anything involving personal guarantees on substantial amounts
- Complex IP licensing or assignment
- Contracts where the other party has significant leverage

The AI review often determines whether legal review is warranted. If the AI surfaces three red flags, that's $300–500 in legal fees well spent. If it finds nothing unusual, you've made an informed decision to skip the legal review.

## Real Examples

**Commercial lease**: A retail business owner uploads a 40-page lease. The AI summarizes: 5-year term, $4,200/month base rent escalating 3% annually, CAM charges estimated at $800/month, personal guarantee required, 60-day notice required for non-renewal. The owner now understands the full financial commitment before negotiating.

**Software vendor agreement**: A service company uploads a SaaS agreement. The AI flags: auto-renewal with 90-day cancellation window, data ownership clause that grants the vendor broad rights to usage data, and a limitation of liability clause capped at one month's fees. The company goes back to negotiate the data clause.

**Contractor agreement**: A consulting firm uploads a subcontractor agreement from a client. The AI identifies: IP assignment clause that assigns all work product to the client, non-solicitation clause lasting 24 months, and payment terms of net-60. The firm negotiates on IP for pre-existing tools and gets net-30 terms.

## How to Get the Most from AI Contract Analysis

**Upload the full document**: Don't summarize or excerpt — give the AI the complete contract.

**Ask specific questions**: "What are the termination provisions?" gets better results than "Summarize this."

**Check for amendments**: Contracts often have addenda or amendments that modify the base terms. Process those separately.

**Use it before negotiations**: Understanding the contract before you negotiate means you're not discovering problems at signing.
