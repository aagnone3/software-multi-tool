Private equity is fundamentally a document business. Every deal starts with a confidential information memorandum (CIM), moves through a data room with thousands of files, and closes with a stack of legal agreements. Between those stages, there are financial models, management presentations, market studies, and reference calls to analyze.

The firms that process this information faster and more accurately win more deals.

AI is becoming a significant advantage in this process.

## The Due Diligence Document Problem

A typical PE deal involves:
- A 50-100 page CIM
- A data room with 500-5,000 documents
- Historical financials going back 3-5 years
- Customer contracts and key vendor agreements
- Employee agreements for key personnel
- Legal documents: corporate history, IP, litigation

For a team of 3-4 associates and a VP, reading all of this carefully in a compressed timeline is genuinely difficult. Things get missed. The pressure to move fast creates risk.

AI doesn't solve the deal judgment problem — deciding whether a company is worth acquiring at a given price still requires human expertise. But AI dramatically accelerates the document-processing step that precedes that judgment.

## How PE Firms Are Using AI in 2025

### 1. CIM Analysis and Initial Screening

AI can read a CIM and produce a structured summary covering:
- Business description and model
- Key financial metrics and trends
- Competitive positioning and market dynamics
- Management team highlights
- Investment thesis and growth opportunities
- Red flags and risk factors

This 15-minute AI summary doesn't replace careful reading — but it gives the deal team a structured starting point and helps identify which deals warrant deeper investigation.

For firms looking at 200+ deals per year, even saving 1-2 hours per CIM review is meaningful.

### 2. Data Room Processing

The data room is where deals get made and lost. AI can help teams:

**Categorize and index documents:** Instead of manually organizing hundreds of files, AI can classify documents by type (financial, legal, operational, HR) and tag them for easy retrieval.

**Extract key financial data:** AI can pull revenue figures, EBITDA, customer concentration, and other metrics from financial statements across multiple years.

**Contract review:** Customer contracts can be analyzed for revenue concentration, renewal terms, termination rights, and pricing structure. Vendor agreements can be flagged for unusual provisions.

**Employment agreement review:** Key executive agreements can be analyzed for change-of-control provisions, non-competes, and compensation structure.

### 3. Portfolio Company Reporting

After close, PE firms manage monthly and quarterly reporting from portfolio companies. AI can:

- Summarize board packages and highlight performance against plan
- Flag metrics that are deviating from budget
- Extract key KPIs from financial reports automatically
- Prepare consistent board materials across portfolio companies

This saves significant associate time and improves consistency across the portfolio.

### 4. Exit Preparation

When preparing a portfolio company for exit, teams need to create comprehensive information packages. AI can help compile and organize data room materials, draft management presentation narratives, and ensure consistency across documents.

## Specific AI Applications That Are Working Well

### Meeting Summarization for Management Presentations

Management presentations and reference calls are critical for PE due diligence. AI meeting summarizers can capture:
- Key questions asked and answers given
- Management commentary on financial performance
- Customer reference call highlights
- Advisor and consultant meeting summaries

This documentation is valuable both during diligence and for post-close portfolio management.

### Document Intelligence for Contract Analysis

Contract analysis tools that can read NDAs, customer agreements, and supplier contracts across a data room in hours rather than weeks. Teams use these to:
- Identify revenue concentration (what percentage is under contract vs. at-will?)
- Spot change-of-control provisions that might require customer notification or consent
- Flag unusual liability or indemnification terms in key agreements

### Financial Document Extraction

AI tools that can extract structured financial data from PDFs, spreadsheets, and management accounts. Instead of manually building a data compilation spreadsheet, teams can use AI to extract and organize financial data across multiple entities and time periods.

## Implementation Approach

**Start with a specific workflow, not a general "AI strategy."** Pick one step in your process — CIM review, reference call summaries, data room categorization — and measure the impact before expanding.

**Build prompt libraries.** The deal teams that get the most value from AI have built standard prompts for common tasks: CIM review, contract analysis, financial summary. This ensures consistency and quality.

**Maintain the paper trail.** Document what AI analyzed and what it flagged. In regulated industries and for LP reporting, having an audit trail of your process matters.

**Train the team.** AI tools are only as good as the users. Associates who know how to ask good questions get dramatically better output than those who don't.

## What AI Won't Replace

**Deal judgment:** Whether a company deserves a premium multiple, whether management is credible, whether the growth thesis is achievable — these are human judgments that require industry experience and pattern recognition.

**Relationship-driven sourcing:** The best deals are often proprietary or semi-proprietary. Relationships with founders, intermediaries, and industry experts are still the primary source of deal flow.

**Negotiation:** AI doesn't negotiate. The term sheet and purchase agreement discussions require human judgment about priorities and trade-offs.

**Integration planning:** Post-close value creation requires operational expertise and relationship management that AI can support but not replace.

## The Competitive Reality

AI adoption in PE is accelerating. Larger funds have dedicated technology teams building proprietary AI tools. Mid-market funds are using off-the-shelf AI platforms. Both groups are getting faster and more thorough.

The question for any PE firm isn't whether to use AI — it's how quickly and how thoughtfully to integrate it into workflows. Firms that figure this out early will have a meaningful advantage in speed and thoroughness, both of which matter in competitive deal processes.

The time savings are real. The risk reduction is real. The question is whether your team is capturing them.
