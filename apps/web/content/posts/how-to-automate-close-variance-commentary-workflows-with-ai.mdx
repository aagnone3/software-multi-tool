Variance commentary is where close often slows down.

The numbers are available, but teams still need clear explanations for what changed, why it changed, and whether follow-up action is required. That writing work is repetitive, and it creates bottlenecks when reviewers are waiting on first drafts.

AI can help by generating structured first-pass commentary while humans keep approval authority.

## What AI should automate in commentary workflows

A high-quality workflow automates the repetitive prep:

1. Pull period-over-period variance data by account/entity
2. Identify material and unusual movements based on defined thresholds
3. Draft concise commentary tied to known drivers
4. Flag low-confidence explanations for manual review
5. Route comments to the right preparer/reviewer queue

Your close process still owns judgment. AI accelerates assembly and triage.

## Build the workflow in four steps

### 1) Standardize your commentary template

Before introducing AI, define a consistent format for every variance note:

- **What changed?** (absolute + % movement)
- **Primary driver(s)**
- **One-time vs recurring**
- **Risk/control impact**
- **Next action owner**

Without a stable template, AI output will be inconsistent and hard to review.

### 2) Feed context, not just balances

Balance deltas alone are not enough. Include context fields such as:

- Cost center or entity
- Known business events (price changes, headcount, timing shifts)
- Prior month commentary for the same account
- Materiality and escalation thresholds

The more structured context you provide, the less speculative the draft commentary becomes.

### 3) Force confidence scoring and escalation tags

Require AI output to include:

- Confidence level (high/medium/low)
- Missing evidence needed
- Escalation flag for reviewer attention

Low-confidence commentary should never auto-approve. It should move into a review queue by default.

### 4) Version and audit every edit

Treat commentary like a controlled close artifact:

- Save AI draft vs human-final versions
- Track who approved the final wording
- Preserve timestamped changes for audit readiness

This preserves control evidence while still capturing speed gains.

## Example output block

- **Account:** 7420 Software Expense
- **Variance:** +$41,500 (+18%)
- **Draft commentary:** Increase driven by annual renewal cycle shifting from Q2 to Q1 plus incremental tool adoption in Operations.
- **Confidence:** Medium
- **Missing support:** Renewal invoice schedule for vendor X
- **Owner:** Close preparer (Ops SG&A)

This format is short, reviewable, and action-oriented.

## Common failure modes

- Generating narrative without thresholds (too much noise)
- Accepting AI text without reviewer sign-off
- Failing to link commentary to source support
- Not reusing approved commentary patterns close-to-close

If you skip controls, you trade speed for audit risk.

## Metrics to track

To prove impact, measure:

- Prep time per commentary cycle
- Reviewer turnaround time
- % of commentary accepted with minor edits
- Number of escalations caught before final close

If cycle time drops while reviewer quality holds, the system is working.

## Final takeaway

Automating close variance commentary workflows with AI is a force multiplier for accounting and FP&A teams.

Use AI to draft and triage. Keep humans responsible for review, approval, and control evidence.

That balance gives you faster close execution without sacrificing governance.
