Fixed asset depreciation should be routine. In practice, it often turns into month-end cleanup.

Teams inherit incomplete asset records, change useful lives without consistent approvals, and reconcile depreciation late. The accounting rules are stable — the process around them is what breaks.

AI helps by standardizing inputs, generating draft schedules, and flagging exceptions before close.

## Where depreciation workflows fail

Most pain points are operational:

- asset metadata is incomplete or inconsistent across systems
- additions/disposals are recorded late
- useful-life and salvage-value updates are not documented clearly
- support files are disconnected from journal entries

Result: manual rework, late adjustments, and avoidable audit follow-ups.

## What AI should automate

Use AI to prepare and validate, not to post unreviewed entries.

High-value automation targets:

1. **Asset data extraction and normalization** from invoices, contracts, and ERP exports
2. **Schedule drafting** using policy rules (method, useful life, convention)
3. **Exception detection** for unusual swings, missing fields, and policy mismatches
4. **Support package generation** linking entries to source documents and approval notes

Keep final approval and posting with designated finance owners.

## A practical rollout plan

### 1) Define one asset intake standard

Every new asset record should include:

- asset class
- acquisition date
- placed-in-service date
- cost basis
- depreciation method and useful life
- salvage value (if applicable)
- owner/cost center
- source document links

Consistency at intake removes the majority of downstream fixes.

### 2) Auto-generate draft depreciation schedules

For each asset, AI can draft:

- periodic depreciation amount
- opening and closing net book value by period
- proration for first/last partial periods
- expected journal entry metadata

Route ambiguous assets (missing service date, contradictory terms) to a review queue.

### 3) Add exception checks before posting

Run policy checks before close:

- missing or invalid useful life
- disposal date before service date
- material variance vs prior-period depreciation trend
- assets with no supporting document links

Escalate exceptions, but let clean items flow.

## Controls that preserve trust

- preparer/approver separation on depreciation adjustments
- version history for useful-life and salvage-value changes
- mandatory reason codes for manual overrides
- traceable links from each entry to source docs and approvals

Without controls, automation can make bad assumptions scale faster.

## Metrics to track over two close cycles

Measure outcomes, not just automation usage:

- time spent preparing depreciation entries
- count of late/manual depreciation adjustments
- reviewer cycle time for exceptions
- audit questions tied to fixed asset support

If these improve, the system is actually reducing risk and effort.

## Bottom line

Fixed asset depreciation is predictable work that often becomes chaotic due to process gaps.

AI is most effective when it standardizes asset data, drafts schedules, and surfaces exceptions early. Keep approval controls strong, and you get faster close cycles without sacrificing audit readiness.
