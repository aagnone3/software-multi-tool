## The Problem with Meeting Recaps

You just wrapped a 45-minute call. You have a page of messy notes, three Slack messages you sent yourself mid-meeting, and a vague memory of who said they'd follow up on the design review.

Now someone's asking for the recap.

Writing meeting summaries is one of those tasks that takes 15–30 minutes and produces something most people skim once and forget. It's pure overhead — necessary, but not valuable.

AI can handle this almost instantly.

## What a Good Meeting Summary Looks Like

Before reaching for a tool, it helps to know what you actually want:

1. **What was decided** — the concrete outcomes, not just what was discussed
2. **Who owns what** — action items with names attached
3. **Key context** — background facts or decisions that someone who missed the call needs to understand
4. **Open questions** — things explicitly left unresolved

A good AI summary should produce all four of these in under 30 seconds.

## Two Inputs That Work Well

### Option 1: Paste Your Raw Notes

If you took notes in real time, they don't need to be clean. You can paste something like:

```
- Jake: push the deadline 2 weeks, need UX review first
- Sarah: confirmed budget approved for Q2
- Action: Jake to schedule UX review by Friday
- Question: do we need legal sign-off before launch?
- Timeline: soft launch early April, hard launch late April
```

And get back:

> **Decisions:** Project deadline pushed 2 weeks pending UX review. Q2 budget approved.
>
> **Action Items:**
> - Jake: Schedule UX review by Friday
>
> **Open Questions:**
> - Legal sign-off requirement before launch

### Option 2: Upload a Transcript

If your call was recorded and auto-transcribed (Zoom, Google Meet, Teams, Otter.ai), you can upload the `.txt`, `.vtt`, or `.srt` file directly. The AI reads the whole thing and extracts signal.

This works especially well for long calls or calls with multiple speakers, where your real-time notes might have missed something.

## Tips for Better Results

**Be specific in your notes.** "We discussed the timeline" is harder to summarize than "Jake: pushing deadline 2 weeks." The more concrete your input, the more concrete the output.

**Include attendees if it matters.** If you write names ("Sarah said X"), action items will be attributed properly instead of left generic.

**For long transcripts, focus on the highlights.** You don't need to upload a full 60-minute transcript if only 20 minutes were substantive. Trim it first.

**Review before sending.** AI summaries are good but not perfect. Scan the action items especially — misattributions happen with messy transcripts.

That's not a rounding error. It's a real workday reclaimed every two weeks.
