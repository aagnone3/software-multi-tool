Most collections teams don’t struggle with sending reminders.
They struggle with sending the *right* reminder at the right escalation level.

That’s where AI helps: better prioritization, better message quality, and better timing — while your policy rules stay deterministic.

## 1) Define your escalation policy before touching AI

AI should not invent your collections policy.

Start with explicit rules:

- Day 1 overdue: friendly reminder
- Day 7 overdue: firmer reminder + payment options
- Day 14 overdue: manager escalation
- Day 21+ overdue: hold account / legal review trigger

Include exceptions for strategic accounts, active disputes, and approved payment plans.

## 2) Build account-level risk segmentation

Not all overdue invoices should be treated equally.

Use AI-assisted scoring inputs like:

- Historical payment behavior
- Days sales outstanding trend by account
- Open dispute volume
- Invoice amount and concentration risk
- Contract type and renewal proximity

Then route accounts into clear buckets (low, medium, high risk) with defined playbooks.

## 3) Personalize reminder content using structured context

Generic reminders get ignored. AI can draft messages using account context:

- Referenced invoice IDs and due dates
- Outstanding balance summary
- Prior communication history
- Available payment methods
- Named internal contact

Require approved templates and legal/compliance language blocks so teams stay on-brand and policy-safe.

## 4) Add dispute-aware suppression rules

One of the fastest ways to damage trust: sending aggressive collections emails while an active billing dispute exists.

Your workflow should:

- Detect dispute keywords or linked dispute tickets
- Pause escalation sequences automatically
- Route the account to dispute resolution track
- Resume dunning only when dispute status is closed

AI classification can help detect this early, but suppression should be deterministic.

## 5) Use AI to recommend next-best action for collectors

Collectors shouldn’t have to re-read 20 threads per account.

Generate a concise “next-best action” card:

- Current escalation stage
- Recommended channel (email, call, exec touch)
- Suggested objective for outreach
- Risk flags (churn, legal, unresolved dispute)

This reduces manual triage and increases consistency across the team.

## 6) Track quality metrics, not just recovery totals

Cash collected matters, but so does process quality.

Monitor:

- Time to first meaningful response
- Promise-to-pay conversion rate
- Escalation stage effectiveness
- Dispute-trigger false positives
- Collection-related churn indicators

AI gives leverage only if you close the loop with weekly process tuning.

## Common mistakes

1. **Over-automating high-risk accounts**
   Keep manual approval gates for large balances or strategic customers.

2. **No tone differentiation by escalation stage**
   If every email sounds the same, escalation has no behavioral effect.

3. **No single source of account truth**
   AI output degrades if invoice, CRM, and support context are fragmented.

4. **Ignoring customer experience impact**
   Collections effectiveness should not come at the cost of avoidable churn.

## Final take

Dunning is not just reminder automation. It’s a controlled escalation system.

With AI, finance teams can prioritize better, communicate more clearly, and recover cash faster — while still enforcing policy, preserving customer trust, and keeping a clean audit trail.
