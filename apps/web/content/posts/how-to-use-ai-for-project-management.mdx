Project managers spend a lot of time on tasks that feel important but don't require judgment: writing meeting summaries, drafting status updates, reviewing contracts, and creating documentation.

AI tools are particularly good at exactly these tasks. They're not good at managing stakeholder relationships, making trade-off decisions, or navigating organizational dynamics — but those aren't the parts that eat your time.

Here's a practical breakdown of where AI helps in project management and where it doesn't.

## Where AI Delivers Real Value

### Meeting Notes and Action Items

Status meetings, sprint reviews, kickoffs, and retrospectives all generate information that needs to be captured and distributed. Writing these notes manually is tedious and often delayed because the PM is tired after a long day of meetings.

AI meeting summarizers can:
- Transcribe recorded meetings automatically
- Extract action items with owners and due dates
- Identify decisions made
- Produce a structured summary ready to send

The output quality is good enough that most PMs use AI-generated notes with minor edits rather than writing from scratch. Time savings: 30-60 minutes per meeting.

### Status Report Drafting

Weekly status reports follow a predictable pattern: what's done, what's in progress, what's blocked, what's coming next. Once you feed an AI tool the right inputs (completed tickets, blockers, upcoming milestones), it can draft these in seconds.

The trick is that AI doesn't know your project context — you need to provide the inputs. But the drafting itself, which usually takes 20-30 minutes, becomes a 5-minute review.

### Contract and Vendor Document Review

Projects involve a lot of documents: vendor contracts, SOWs, SLAs, MSAs. Reading these carefully takes hours. AI contract analysis tools can extract:
- Key obligations and deliverables
- Payment terms and milestones
- Liability and termination clauses
- Data handling and security requirements

This doesn't replace legal review, but it gives PMs a quick summary before detailed review and flags sections that need attention.

### Project Documentation

Technical specs, process documentation, onboarding guides — these need to be written but rarely require specialized expertise. AI can draft first versions from bullet points or meeting notes, which are then edited rather than written from scratch.

## Practical Workflow Examples

### Before Every Status Meeting

1. Pull completed and in-progress items from your project management tool
2. Note any blockers and upcoming milestones
3. Feed into an AI tool with a simple prompt: "Draft a status update based on these project notes"
4. Edit and send

Total time: 10 minutes instead of 25.

### After Every Meeting

1. Record the meeting (with participant permission)
2. Upload to an AI meeting summarizer
3. Review the generated action items
4. Distribute with minor edits

Total time: 5 minutes instead of 20.

### When Reviewing Vendor Documents

1. Upload the contract/SOW to an AI document analyzer
2. Ask it to extract key terms, obligations, and any unusual clauses
3. Use the summary for initial review
4. Do detailed reading on flagged sections

Total time: 30 minutes instead of 2 hours for a typical 20-page contract.

## What AI Doesn't Help With

**Stakeholder management**: Navigating organizational politics, managing difficult personalities, and building trust relationships require human judgment.

**Scope decisions**: When something needs to be cut, AI can present options but can't make the trade-off for you.

**Risk assessment**: AI can list common project risks but doesn't understand your specific organizational context.

**Prioritization with incomplete information**: The most important PM skill — making good decisions without full information — isn't something AI tools handle well.

## Getting Started Without Changing Your Workflow

The lowest-friction entry point is meeting summarization. Pick your next recurring meeting, record it, and try an AI summarizer. Compare the output to what you would have written. If it's 80% there with 5 minutes of editing, you've found a sustainable time save.

From there, add contract review when the next vendor document comes in, and status report drafting when the next weekly update is due.

Don't try to AI-ify everything at once. Find the two or three places where the time savings are most reliable and build from there.

## The Right Mental Model

Think of AI tools as a junior assistant who is fast and accurate with formatting and summarization, but needs your direction on what matters and what to do with the output.

You give it the inputs, it produces a first draft, you edit to add context and judgment. That's the productive collaboration pattern — not replacing your thinking, but removing the parts that don't require your thinking.
