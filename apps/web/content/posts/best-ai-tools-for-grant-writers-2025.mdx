Grant writing is research-intensive work: reviewing funder RFPs, synthesizing program reports and evaluation data, tracking budget documentation, and pulling together supporting evidence. The writing itself requires expertise — but a significant portion of the preparation work is reading and extraction.

AI tools are helping grant writers spend less time on preparation and more time on the strategic arguments that win awards.

## The Time Problem in Grant Writing

A grant writer preparing a competitive federal or foundation proposal often spends:
- 3–5 hours reviewing the RFP and eligibility requirements
- 2–4 hours pulling supporting data from program reports and evaluations
- 2–4 hours reviewing financial documents and budget narratives from prior years
- Several hours coordinating with program staff, finance teams, and leadership

Much of that is reading, extracting, and organizing. AI can help with all of it.

## How Grant Writers Are Using AI

### Analyzing RFPs and Funding Announcements

Federal grant notices (NOFAs, FOAs) can run 50–100 pages. Foundation RFPs vary widely but still require careful review for eligibility requirements, evaluation criteria weighting, narrative instructions, and budget allowances.

AI document analysis tools can:
- Extract eligibility requirements and flag potential disqualifiers
- Summarize evaluation criteria and their relative weights
- Pull out budget limitations, matching requirements, and allowable expenses
- Compare requirements across multiple funding opportunities to prioritize pursuit

### Summarizing Program Reports and Evaluation Data

Successful grant applications draw on evidence from prior programs — outcome data, evaluation findings, participant stories. AI summarization can turn a 40-page evaluation report into a concise evidence summary that your narrative can draw from.

This is particularly valuable when you're pulling evidence from multiple prior-year reports or when program staff have submitted lengthy narrative updates that need to be condensed.

### Processing Financial Documentation

Grant budgets require reconciliation against prior award actuals, audit reports, and financial statements. AI invoice and financial document processing can extract relevant figures and surface the numbers you need for budget narratives and fiscal capacity documentation.

### Organizing Supporting Documentation

Grant applications often require assembling 20–30 attachments — board lists, IRS determination letters, financial statements, letters of support, resumes, evaluation plans. AI can help organize, label, and cross-reference supporting documents against requirements checklists.

## What AI Does Well (and Doesn't)

AI is strong at:
- Reading large documents quickly and extracting specific information
- Summarizing technical reports into narrative-friendly language
- Organizing and categorizing documents
- Spotting inconsistencies between documents (e.g., budget figures that don't match narratives)

AI is weaker at:
- Understanding the strategic positioning that differentiates proposals
- Knowing which evidence will resonate with a specific program officer
- Writing compelling program narratives without significant human input
- Replacing the relationship knowledge that experienced grant writers bring

Use AI to handle the reading and organization work so you can spend more time on strategy and writing.

## Getting Started

Start with RFP analysis — upload a recent funding announcement and ask an AI tool to extract eligibility requirements, evaluation criteria, and budget parameters. Compare the output against your manual read.

If the quality is good, apply it to summarizing prior program reports. Build from there.

The goal isn't to automate grant writing — it's to eliminate the preparation work that doesn't require expertise, so more of your time goes into the parts that do.
