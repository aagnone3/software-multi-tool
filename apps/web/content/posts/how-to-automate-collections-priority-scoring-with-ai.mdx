Most collections teams have more follow-ups than available hours.

If your queue is sorted only by invoice age, collectors spend time on the wrong accounts while higher-impact risks sit untouched.

AI priority scoring helps you rank accounts by expected impact so your team works the right list first.

## 1) Define what “priority” should optimize

Start with explicit outcomes:

- Fastest cash recovery
- Lowest write-off risk
- Best use of collector time
- Protection of strategic customer relationships

Your score should reflect these priorities—not just one dimension like days past due.

## 2) Build a practical feature set from existing AR data

Use inputs you already trust:

- Days past due
- Open balance and invoice count
- Historical payment behavior (on-time vs late)
- Promise-to-pay reliability
- Dispute frequency
- Customer tier or revenue importance
- Collector touch history and response rate

You do not need a perfect data warehouse to begin; a stable, explainable feature set is enough.

## 3) Use tiered scoring instead of one opaque number

Make results operationally clear:

- **Tier 1 (urgent):** high balance + high risk + low responsiveness
- **Tier 2 (high):** meaningful exposure with moderate risk
- **Tier 3 (routine):** lower exposure or highly reliable payers

Collectors should immediately know how to act from the tier, not interpret a black-box score.

## 4) Add next-best-action recommendations per tier

A score without action creates another dashboard.

Attach suggested actions such as:

- Call + escalation email today
- Personalized reminder with payment-link CTA
- Promise-to-pay confirmation workflow
- Dispute-resolution handoff
- Watchlist follow-up in 72 hours

This turns prioritization into execution.

## 5) Recompute scores daily (or intra-day for high volume)

Priority changes quickly when customers reply, pay, or dispute.

Set a refresh cadence that matches your volume:

- Daily batch scoring for small/medium teams
- Multiple refreshes per day for larger AR operations

Always include timestamped “score as of” metadata for auditability.

## 6) Keep human overrides visible and measurable

Collectors should be able to override rank when they have context.

Track overrides by reason:

- Strategic account sensitivity
- Known upcoming payment
- Billing error in progress
- Customer contact unavailable

Then review override patterns monthly to improve your scoring logic.

## 7) Measure queue quality, not just model accuracy

Operational KPIs matter more than abstract model metrics:

- Cash collected per collector-hour
- Reduction in aged receivables buckets
- Promise-to-pay conversion rate
- Escalation-to-resolution time
- Write-off rate by score tier

If these improve, the system is working.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Scoring accounts once and never refreshing
- Hiding logic from collectors (“just trust the model”)
- Ignoring relationship-sensitive enterprise accounts
- Optimizing for contact volume instead of cash outcomes
- Treating all disputes as equal risk

## Final takeaway

AI collections priority scoring works when it is transparent, action-oriented, and tightly connected to AR outcomes.

Start with simple tiers, daily refreshes, and clear next actions. Then iterate using collector feedback and cash-impact metrics—not model vanity scores.
