Most teams only review customer credit limits when something goes wrong.

A payment is missed, exposure is already high, and finance has to choose between slowing revenue or accepting more risk.

AI helps move this from reactive firefighting to a consistent weekly process.

## 1) Define review triggers before introducing automation

Set objective triggers that force a review:

- Utilization above 80% of current credit limit
- Days sales outstanding (DSO) trend worsening over 2+ billing cycles
- Overdue balance above a set threshold
- Sudden spend increase versus historical baseline
- External risk signal change (if available)

Without explicit triggers, AI just scales inconsistency.

## 2) Build a simple risk score for every account

You do not need a complex model first.

Start with weighted inputs such as:

- Recent payment timeliness
- Average days late over last 90 days
- Concentration risk (large open balance relative to portfolio)
- Dispute frequency
- Revenue trajectory and account tenure

Use AI to summarize these inputs into a short recommendation:

- Increase limit
- Keep limit unchanged
- Decrease limit
- Escalate for manual review

## 3) Separate decisioning from communication

Keep these as two workflows:

1. **Decision workflow:** score + recommendation + confidence level
2. **Communication workflow:** customer-facing message and internal notes

This prevents accidental customer communication from draft internal rationale and keeps approvals auditable.

## 4) Add policy guardrails and human checkpoints

AI should recommend, not unilaterally override policy.

Use clear controls:

- Max auto-increase percentage per review cycle
- Mandatory manager approval above threshold changes
- Auto-block if account has unresolved disputes
- Auto-escalate when confidence is below threshold

Guardrails make automation safer and easier to trust.

## 5) Run weekly review batches, not ad hoc one-offs

Batching creates operational rhythm and cleaner reporting.

Each week:

- Generate the triggered account list
- Produce AI recommendations + explanations
- Route edge cases to analyst queue
- Publish approved changes with an audit trail

This keeps credit review predictable and reduces month-end surprises.

## Metrics that prove it’s working

Track a tight scorecard:

- % of accounts reviewed on schedule
- Median time from trigger to decision
- Bad-debt rate by decision category
- Manual-review rate (and why)
- Revenue unlocked from approved limit increases

If manual-review rate is too high, tighten trigger quality and recommendation confidence thresholds.

## Common mistakes

1. **No policy floor/ceiling**
   Fast recommendations become risky decisions.

2. **Overweighting one signal**
   A single late payment should not dominate every case.

3. **No explanation requirement**
   If a reviewer cannot understand “why,” they will ignore the system.

4. **No post-change monitoring**
   Limit increases should be monitored for 30–60 days to validate outcomes.

## Final take

Credit limit reviews should be proactive, explainable, and routine.

With AI, finance teams can review more accounts with better consistency, reduce avoidable exposure, and still approve growth for strong customers faster.
