Most AR teams don’t have a reminder problem.
They have a prioritization problem.

When every overdue account lands in one queue, collectors spend too much time on low-impact tasks while higher-risk balances keep aging.

## 1) Define what “priority” means for your business

Before using AI, choose the business outcomes you care about:

- Cash recovery speed
- Reduction in aging balances (60/90+ days)
- Lower write-off risk
- Retention of strategic customers

If your team can’t explain the objective, the model will optimize noise.

## 2) Build a deterministic baseline score first

Start with clear rules, then let AI improve the ranking.

Example baseline factors:

- Days overdue
- Invoice amount
- Number of open invoices
- Broken promise-to-pay history
- Existing dispute status

This gives you a transparent fallback if AI confidence is low.

## 3) Add AI context signals that humans miss at scale

AI is useful when it can digest unstructured history quickly.

Add features like:

- Sentiment from prior customer replies
- Language that signals payment friction (approval delays, budget holds)
- Frequency of stalled threads
- Churn-risk hints from support or success notes
- Contract-renewal proximity

Use these as *ranking inputs*, not autonomous decisions.

## 4) Segment queue into action lanes

One ranked list is still hard to execute. Split into lanes with playbooks:

- **Lane A: High value + high risk** → senior collector same day
- **Lane B: Medium risk** → structured multi-step sequence
- **Lane C: Low risk / low value** → automated reminders + monitor
- **Lane D: Dispute-linked** → route to dispute workflow, not collections

Collectors should see “why this account is here,” not just a score.

## 5) Generate account briefings for each follow-up

Instead of forcing reps to read long email chains, auto-generate a briefing:

- Outstanding balance and oldest invoice
- Last customer response and intent summary
- Known blockers (approval, dispute, PO mismatch)
- Recommended next message objective

This reduces prep time and improves consistency across team members.

## 6) Keep policy guardrails non-negotiable

Priority automation should not bypass governance.

Keep deterministic controls for:

- Escalation thresholds
- Legal/account hold triggers
- Communication compliance language
- Strategic-account approval paths

AI should suggest and rank, while policy decides final eligibility.

## 7) Measure whether prioritization is actually improving outcomes

Track pre/post metrics by lane:

- Collector touches per recovered dollar
- Time to first meaningful response
- Percentage of balances resolved before 60+ days
- Promise-to-pay conversion rate
- Write-off trend

If score complexity increases but these metrics don’t improve, simplify.

## Common mistakes

1. **Treating score as truth**
   Scores are probability estimates, not policy overrides.

2. **No feedback loop from collector outcomes**
   If you never feed outcomes back, model quality plateaus quickly.

3. **Mixing disputes into collections priority**
   Disputes need a separate path; combining them inflates false urgency.

4. **Over-optimizing for invoice amount only**
   Large balance ≠ highest recoverability. Include behavior and risk context.

## Final take

Collections performance improves when teams focus on the right accounts first.

AI can materially improve that focus by turning account history into better queue ranking and clearer next actions — as long as your policy rules stay deterministic and your outcomes are measured weekly.
