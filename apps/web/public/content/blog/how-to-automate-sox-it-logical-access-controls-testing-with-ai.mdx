SOX IT logical access controls testing is one of the most repetitive — and highest-risk — areas of a compliance program. Auditors spend hours pulling user access lists, comparing them against role definitions, and documenting exceptions. And every year, the scope seems to grow.

AI tools are starting to change that equation. Here's a practical look at where they help most.

## What Makes Logical Access Controls Testing So Painful

Logical access controls (LAC) testing under SOX involves verifying that only authorized users can access sensitive systems and data. In practice, that means:

- Extracting user access reports from ERP, HRIS, and financial systems
- Matching access to approved role matrices
- Identifying terminated employees who still have active accounts
- Flagging segregation of duties (SOD) conflicts
- Documenting evidence for each finding and exception

For a mid-sized company with multiple systems, this can easily consume two to three weeks of audit time — twice a year.

## Where AI Fits In

### 1. Automated Access Report Analysis

AI document analysis tools can ingest exported user access reports (CSV, PDF, Excel) and instantly compare them against a baseline role matrix. Instead of manually reviewing hundreds of rows, you get a flagged exception list in minutes.

**What to do:** Export your user access report and role matrix as separate files, then run them through an AI analysis tool with a prompt like: *"Identify users with access that doesn't match their approved role, and flag any terminated employees with active accounts."*

### 2. SOD Conflict Detection

Segregation of duties conflicts — where one person has both "create" and "approve" rights for the same transaction — are notoriously hard to catch manually across multiple systems. AI can cross-reference access rights across systems and flag conflicts against a predefined SOD rule set.

**What to do:** Define your SOD conflict rules once (e.g., "no user should have both AP entry and AP approval access"). Then apply them automatically to every quarterly pull.

### 3. Terminated Employee Access Reviews

The window between an employee's last day and account deactivation is one of the most common audit findings. AI can automate the comparison between your HR termination log and active directory exports to flag gaps in near real time.

**What to do:** Schedule a weekly automated comparison rather than waiting for the quarterly audit cycle. Catch and close gaps proactively.

### 4. Evidence Documentation

Once exceptions are identified, AI can help draft the standard workpaper documentation: what was found, the population tested, the sampling approach, and the conclusion. This cuts the write-up time per finding by 60–80%.

**What to do:** Use a document summarization tool to generate a structured exception summary from your raw findings list, then review and finalize rather than writing from scratch.

### 5. Recurring Review Scheduling

Access reviews shouldn't happen only during audit season. AI-assisted scheduling tools can trigger quarterly access certification workflows, route them to the right approvers, and track completion status — creating a continuous controls environment rather than a point-in-time snapshot.

## What AI Won't Do For You

- Replace human judgment on materiality and risk classification
- Automatically remediate access issues (that requires system admin action)
- Guarantee audit acceptance — AI output still needs review by your controls team
- Substitute for a well-defined role matrix and SOD ruleset (garbage in, garbage out)

## Getting Started

If you're new to using AI for LAC testing, start with the most manual part of your current process. Usually that's the user access report comparison. Run a pilot on one system, document the time savings, then expand from there.

The goal isn't to automate everything at once. It's to remove the repetitive parts so your team can focus on what actually requires judgment: evaluating risk, documenting context, and making the call on borderline findings.
